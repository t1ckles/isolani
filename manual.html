SECTION 01
THE GALAXY AND YOUR PLACE IN IT

The galaxy is not what it was. This is not a complaint. It is a survey finding, and the Guild records survey findings without editorializing. What the data shows is a civilization in structural decline: fewer stations operational, more systems running dark, infrastructure failing faster than it is being replaced. The Guild has been documenting this trend for approximately forty years. The trend has not reversed.

Your role as an independent contractor is to operate within this environment — to move through space that larger institutions cannot efficiently reach, to gather data and commodities that the network needs, and to survive long enough to file your reports. The Guild does not require you to fix the galaxy. It requires you to chart it accurately.

Galaxy Structure

The known galaxy is organized into eight quadrants, each named and each in a distinct state of civilization. Quadrants contain clusters, which contain individual star systems. Every galaxy is procedurally generated from a seed — a four-part code assigned at registration. The seed determines everything: system names, station locations, faction presence, veydrite deposits, hazard ratings. Two pilots with different seeds inhabit different galaxies.

The seed is not metaphorical. The Guild's deep mapping engines require a starting value from which to derive the full structure of local space. In practice, this means that no two registration events produce the same galaxy. The contractor who registered before you and the contractor who registers after you are each alone in their own version of the dark. The Guild finds this philosophically unremarkable.

Quadrant States

Each quadrant carries a civilization state that determines station density, encounter frequency, veydrite pricing, faction mix, and hazard levels. States are not static over geological time but are stable over the operational period of a single contractor. Do not expect them to change.

The Xeno Question

The Guild's official position is that certain anomalous survey findings remain under active review. This document will not elaborate further. Contractors operating in systems flagged with anomalous sensor returns are advised to complete their survey quickly and file their data. The Guild is particularly interested in astrographic records from such systems. The premium reflects this interest.

FIELD NOTE If something in a system does not resolve on sensors, do not attempt repeated resolution sweeps. Log the anomaly. Leave the system. File your data. The Guild will handle classification. You are a surveyor, not an investigator.
SECTION 02
THE ASSAYER'S GUILD

The Assayer's Guild is the oldest continuously operating institution in known space. It predates the current collapse cycle by several centuries and has survived every previous one. Its longevity is not accidental. The Guild survives because every other faction — Pelk Logistics, Colonial Colonies Command, feral settlements, independent operators — needs what the Guild provides: neutral monetary infrastructure and authoritative cartographic records. The Guild does not take sides. It takes percentages.

The Guild's neutrality is its entire value. The moment it favors one corp over another, it loses the thing that makes the galaxy function around it. This is why Guild stations are the most common copy-known this. The Guild knows every major corp knows this. This mutual understanding is the glue that holds the galaxy together — or rather, holds its pieces in roughly the same relative positions.

What the Guild Does

The Guild performs three core functions: it maintains the monetary exchange system (Veydrite Dust pricing, Corp Scrip exchange, currency certification), it maintains the cartographic record (astrographic data collection, star chart distribution, survey certification), and it maintains the contractor network (registration, contract facilitation, reputation tracking).

As a registered independent contractor, you operate on Guild-issued credentials. Your terminal connects to the Guild network. Your astrographic data sells to Guild archive stations. Your reputation score is maintained in Guild ledgers. You are, in a meaningful sense, a Guild instrument — one they have deployed into space at no cost to themselves, because you bear all the operational risk.

The Guild does not think of contractors this way, officially. Officially, contractors are valued independent partners whose contributions to the cartographic record are essential to Guild operations. This is also true. Both things can be true simultaneously. The Guild is comfortable with this.

The Assay Terminal

The terminal interface you are currently operating is a Guild-issue Deep Survey Terminal, designation v0.2.0-dev. It provides navigation, sensor, trade, combat, and record-keeping functions through a command-line interface. The terminal connects to Guild network nodes at registered stations. When you are not docked, you are operating on cached data and local sensors only.

The Auspex panel — the secondary display to the right of the main terminal — provides real-time local grid information: system composition, station presence, signals, environmental hazard, and traffic data. It updates on navigation events and manual survey commands. It does not update on every keystroke. Sensor time is not free.

SECTION 03
CURRENCY AND ECONOMY

Veydrite Dust

Veydrite Dust is the bedrock commodity of the known economy. It occurs in trace quantities around certain stellar classes — O and B-class stars produce the richest deposits — and is extracted through field salvage operations. Veydrite does not corrode, does not decay, cannot be synthesized, and has verified mass. These properties make it the only universally accepted store of value in a galaxy where institutional trust is eroding along with everything else.

The Guild sets the base Veydrite exchange rate. This rate varies by quadrant state: Established space pays less because supply is more reliable; Collapsed space pays more because getting there is genuinely dangerous. Your Guild standing also affects the rate you receive — TRUSTED contractors receive a 15% premium, HOSTILE contractors receive a 25% penalty, and will find Guild stations refusing them entry regardless.

Corp Scrip

Corp Scrip is the transactional layer — the local currency used for day-to-day purchases. It is issued and backstopped by the dominant corporate presence in a given region. A regional corp's "scrip" is essentially a tax-bill formatted as paper. Scrip buys fuel. Scrip pays docking fees. Scrip denominates contract payments. The Assayer's Guild certifies exchange rates between competing scrip systems. This mutual understanding allows trade to continue even in Contested and Declining space.

The fine detail is that this makes the Guild the institution that determines how much any given corp's money is worth relative to any other corp's money. The Guild has never weaponized this power. Every faction in known space spends considerable political energy ensuring the Guild never has reason to.

Fuel

Fuel is measured in units and consumed by nav and jump operations. Nav jumps cost 8–16 units depending on distance and drive efficiency. Blind cluster jumps cost 6–11 units. Salvage operations consume power rather than fuel, but fuel reserves still determine your operational range. Running dry in deep space is a death sentence. Running low is a warning.

The monetary and cartographic authority. Operates the network you are connected to. Buying and selling through Guild stations builds standing. TRUSTED status improves veydrite rates significantly. Pelk Logistics

The dominant commercial freight and logistics operation. Pelk stations are common in Contested and Declining space. They post delivery and interdiction contracts. SECTION 05
NAVIGATION AND TRAVERSAL

Movement between systems is the foundation of contractor operations. There are two transit modes available to registered vessels: calculated nav transit and blind cluster jump. Both consume fuel and power. Both advance the day counter. Plan accordingly.

Nav Transit — nav <system name>

Nav transit allows travel to any named system in the galaxy. The terminal calculates a course, deducts fuel, drains a small amount of power for navigation computation, and advances the day counter by 4–11 days depending on distance. On arrival, the system is available for survey, salvage, and — if a station is present — docking.

Nav transit has an encounter check on arrival. Quadrant state determines base encounter probability. Hostile faction standing increases it. Xeno-tainted systems have a chance of unknown contact regardless of quadrant state.

FUEL WARNING The terminal will refuse nav transit if fuel is insufficient. It will not warn you that you are approaching the margin — it will simply refuse when you attempt transit. Monitor your fuel reserves actively. The sidebar displays current fuel at all times.

Blind Cluster Jump — jump <system name>

Cluster jumps allow rapid transit within your current cluster without full nav computation. They cost less fuel (6–11 units vs 8–16) and fewer days (1–3 vs 4–11), but carry additional risks: a 30% chance of minor hull stress on exit, and an elevated encounter probability due to the uncomputed arrival vector. Useful for rapid local traversal. Not recommended as a primary transit method in high-hazard clusters.

The technical distinction between nav transit and blind jump is the presence or absence of pre-computed exit vector correction. Nav transit plots the exit vector before initiating. Blind jump trusts the drive's internal gyroscopic compensation. The drive is good at this. It is not perfect.

Scanning Before Travel

Before committing to a destination, use scan <1-8> to survey a quadrant. The scan display shows every system in the quadrant with its star class, beacon status, and — if you have previously deepscanned the system — a ◆ charted marker indicating that detailed data is on file. Use this to identify targets before spending fuel.

Encounters

Encounters trigger on nav and jump arrival. Attacker types reflect local conditions:

The Sidebar

The right panel displays persistent ship status. It shows vessel name, captain identity, current day, hull/fuel/power bars, scrip balance, veydrite hold, astrographic chart count, active contract, and faction standing. It updates after every command. Monitor it. It is the difference between knowing you have 20% hull and discovering you have 20% hull when the next shot lands.

The lower-right panel shows local grid data for your current system. It populates on where, nav, dock, and undock commands. It shows system name and type, body list, station presence, signal status, environmental hazard, and traffic contacts once sensors have been swept. It does not auto-update on unrelated commands.

SECTION 08
SURVEY OPERATIONS

Survey operations are the core of contractor function. The Guild needs accurate, current astrographic data. You generate it. The exchange is simple: you take the risk, they take the data, you get paid. The memory matrices aboard your vessel store this data until you elect to sell it or lose it.

Basic Scan — scan <1-8>

Passive sensor sweep of a numbered quadrant. Returns the name and star class of every system in that quadrant, plus beacon presence where detected. Does not generate astrographic data. Does not reveal hazard rating, traffic, station presence, or veydrite. It tells you what exists. Nothing more.

Systems you have previously deepscanned display a ◆ marker indicating chart data is on file. Systems where you have sold that data display no marker — the knowledge left with the sale.

Deep Survey — deepscan <system name>

Full astrographic survey of a named system within your current quadrant. Costs 40 Power Core units. The terminal prints initialization text, then resolves after a variable delay of 3–6 seconds as the array focuses. On completion, the full system profile is displayed and astrographic data is entered into the memory matrix.

Range is limited to your current quadrant. You cannot deepscan systems in other quadrants from a distance. You must be there, or close to there.

Cluster Survey — clusterdeepscan <cluster name>

Initiates sequential deepscan of all systems in a named cluster within your current quadrant. Costs 25 Power Core units per system. The sweep processes each system individually — targeting text, progress messages, and full system output appear in sequence as the array works through the cluster. Power drains and the sidebar updates after each system resolves, not at the end of the sweep.

If power runs low mid-sweep, the operation scans as many systems as possible while maintaining a minimum 1-unit power reserve. The terminal reports how many systems were skipped at completion. You will never be left with a dead core by a cluster sweep.

The cluster sweep is not a shortcut. It takes real time — 3 to 6 seconds per system, more for larger clusters. The array is doing genuine work. What you are watching is not a loading screen. It is your ship performing thirty minutes of sensor labor in compressed time. The data you get at the end is worth it.

Chart Management — charts / charts <system name>

charts displays all astrographic records currently held in your memory matrix — system name, scan quality, day recorded, and unit count. charts <system name> displays the full stored profile for a specific system without leaving the terminal or spending power. This is how you consult data you have already gathered.

Selling Astrographic Data

Astrographic data sells exclusively to Assayer's Guild stations. The Guild is the cartographic authority — other factions do not maintain the archive infrastructure to purchase and process survey data. At a Guild station, enter the trade terminal and use sell astrographics to view your portfolio with current values.

Pricing is calculated per record based on: quadrant state of the surveyed system (Forbidden space pays most), scan quality (deep scan pays full rate, basic pays 30%), data age (very slow decay — approximately 1% per 10 days, floor at 50%), and your Guild standing. Records marked [aging] in the portfolio display have fallen below 85% of original value.

IMPORTANT — MEMORY MATRIX PERMANENCE Sold astrographic data is permanently deleted from your vessel's memory matrix. The ◆ charted marker disappears from scan displays. You will not have the data. The Guild will have the data. If you need to survey that system again, you will need to deepscan it again. Price accordingly.
SECTION 09
SALVAGE OPERATIONS

Field salvage is the primary source of Veydrite Dust for contractors who do not have pre-established extraction contracts. It is conducted using the Harrow-7 Salvage Head utility, which is installed on your vessel at registration. Salvage consumes Power Core units and advances the day counter by 1–2 days per operation.

Initiating Salvage — salvage

Salvage is available in systems containing ruin sites, veydrite deposits, or debris fields. The terminal checks for these automatically. If nothing salvageable is present, the operation is declined. Each system can be salvaged once per visit — move on and return another day, or travel to a new system.

Salvage yields Veydrite Dust and auto-liquidated scrap value. Hazard level affects yield variance and the probability of an incident during the operation. High-hazard systems yield more — if you survive the operation. Xeno-tainted systems have a chance of anomalous finds that do not match any known catalogue.

Ruin Logs — scan log

Ruin sites in a system can be scanned for data logs using scan log. This does not require power — it requires a ruin to be present. Recovered logs are added to your record archive and viewable with logs. Logs are flavor artifacts: crew records, manifests, station closure notices, personal accounts. They do not have direct mechanical value. They have the other kind.

The Guild does not officially assign value to recovered personal logs. They are not catalogued, not purchased, and not filed. Several contractors have noted that the Guild's data archive, which covers approximately forty years of network operation, contains no personal correspondence whatsoever. The Guild has not commented on this observation.

Deadlines and Feasibility

Contract deadlines are generated based on estimated travel time to the target system, plus time for any required scan operations, plus a 20% margin. The deadline is tight but achievable. A contract asking for deep survey of a system six quadrants away with a 3-day deadline is not in the system — the generator enforces feasibility. If a contract looks difficult, it is difficult. It is not impossible.

Speed bonuses apply when you complete a contract within 60% of the allotted time. The bonus is 10% additional payment plus 3 extra reputation points. Getting there fast matters.

Completion and Abandonment

Complete a contract with complete when you are physically present in the target system. Abandoning with abandon applies a -15 reputation penalty with the posting faction. Contract failure (deadline exceeded during transit) is detected automatically and also applies the penalty. The Guild notes all of these events. They do not editorialize.

Weapon Condition

Weapons degrade with use. Condition runs from 0 to 100 and passes through four tiers: NOMINAL (70+), WORN (40–69), DEGRADED (20–39), CRITICAL (below 20). Jam probability increases sharply at each threshold. The terminal notifies you when a weapon crosses a tier boundary during combat. A jammed weapon is unusable for that exchange. A CRITICAL weapon at 55% jam probability is a liability, not an asset. Repair at stations.

Death and Permadeath

Hull reaching zero is death. Your save file is deleted. Your pilot record is entered into the graveyard. There is no continue from last checkpoint. There is no reload. The void does not give back.

The Guild records the death of every registered contractor. The record includes cause of death, day of death, last known location, and achievement log. This information is retained in the Guild archive indefinitely. The Guild does not consider this morbid. It considers it accurate.

SECTION 13
ASTROGRAPHICS — CHART MANAGEMENT

Astrographic data is your primary survey commodity. Unlike Veydrite Dust, which has physical mass and must be extracted and transported, astrographic data exists as structured information in your vessel's memory matrix — a data storage medium of Guild specification, built to survive the conditions of deep space operation.

The Guild specifies memory matrix construction standards for a reason. Early contractor-grade data storage was magnetic or flash-based — both susceptible to the radiation environment of deep space, both prone to gradual corruption over extended missions. The modern Guild-spec matrix uses crystalline data encoding with redundant verification checksums. Data recorded on Day 1 of a mission is as readable on Day 500 as on Day 1, though its market value reflects the passage of time regardless of its physical integrity.

Data Decay

Astrographic data loses approximately 1% of its market value per 10 days. The floor is 50% of base value — old data is worth something, but not full price. The [aging] tag in your chart portfolio appears when a record has fallen below 85% of original value. Sell data when it is fresh if maximum return is the goal. Hold it if you need to consult it for navigation or contract purposes.

SECTION 14
THE GUILD RECORD ARCHIVE

The Guild Record Archive is the longitudinal operational record of a registered contractor. It logs notable events — first operations, standing changes, combat outcomes, survey milestones, economic achievements — and retains them for the duration of the contractor's active registration and beyond. The archive does not delete entries. It only adds them.

Access the archive in-game with the record command. Access it from the main menu via the Guild Record Archive link. Records are organized by category and expand on click to display full detail including the day the event occurred.

The Guild maintains these records not out of sentiment, but because they constitute the most accurate longitudinal dataset of independent contractor operations in known space. Contractors have occasionally asked why the Guild cares how many systems they have charted or whether they survived a combat engagement at 15% hull. The Guild's answer is consistent: accurate data is always useful. The galaxy is large. The Guild wants to know what is in it, and who managed to come back.

The Graveyard

When a contractor dies, their record is finalized and transferred to the permanent archive. Their vessel's last known position, cause of death, day count, and complete achievement log are preserved. Dead pilots are not forgotten. They are filed. There is a distinction, and the Guild considers it important.

SECTION 15
SURVIVAL NOTES

This section contains operational guidance that does not fit neatly into categorical sections. It is guidance the Guild has extracted from contractor debrief records, incident reports, and post-mortem analysis. Most of it is obvious in retrospect.

Power Management

Dock when your core is low. Shore power is free with the docking fee. Running a deep survey operation on 35 power will not work. Running into an encounter on 10 power will end badly. Plan power usage before committing to operations.

Hull Monitoring

The hull bar in the sidebar turns orange below 30%. This is a warning, not a safe threshold. At 20% hull, a single missed evade or a bad combat exchange ends your pilot. Repair early. Repair often. Repair before going into contested space, not after.

Fuel Discipline

Know your fuel state before plotting long courses. The terminal will not warn you that a destination is close to your fuel limit — it will simply refuse transit when you attempt it. If you are in Collapsed space with 12 fuel units and the nearest station is 15 fuel units away, you will need to salvage your way to enough scrip to purchase fuel from whatever Feral settlement is nearby. This is survivable. It is not enjoyable.

Combat Caution

You start with a PDT-4 Point Defense Turret and 50 AP rounds. This is sufficient to handle Feral raiders with good play. It is not sufficient to take on a Pirate Corvette at full health with aggressive confidence. Know your loadout. Know your enemy. profile before engaging. Use sigint to understand subsystem vulnerabilities before deciding where to aim.

Contract Deadlines

Contracts have deadlines. The deadline is calculated based on realistic travel time plus a 20% margin. This means the margin exists, but it is not generous. Do not accept a contract and then spend four days doing salvage runs in the opposite direction. The deadline clock does not care about your detours.

Reputation Drift

Reputation with factions you do not interact with trends toward neutral very slowly. Reputation with factions you repeatedly antagonize does not recover on its own. If you need to repair standing with a faction, the only method is repeated positive interactions — docking, paying fees, completing contracts. There is no shortcut.

The Xeno Presence

Some systems will present readings that do not resolve. Some contacts will not identify. Some logs will describe things that should not be there. The Guild's advice is consistent: log it, file it, leave. The Guild is specifically interested in this data and pays accordingly. Your curiosity, however understandable, is not worth your pilot.

The Guild has reviewed 847 contractor incident reports involving anomalous sensor returns over the past forty years. Of these, 312 involved the contractor attempting extended engagement with the anomaly — additional sensor sweeps, proximity approach, manual hull inspection of anomalous structures. The outcome data for this subset is not published. The Guild considers the aggregate survival rate sufficient guidance.
