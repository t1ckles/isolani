SECTION 01
THE GALAXY AND YOUR PLACE IN IT

The galaxy is not what it was. This is not a complaint. It is a survey finding, and the Guild records survey findings without editorializing. What the data shows is a civilization in structural decline: fewer stations operational, more systems running dark, infrastructure failing faster than it is being replaced. The Guild has been documenting this trend for approximately forty years. The trend has not reversed.

Your role as an independent contractor is to operate within this environment — to move through space that larger institutions cannot efficiently reach, to gather data and commodities that the network needs, and to survive long enough to file your reports. The Guild does not require you to fix the galaxy. It requires you to chart it accurately.

Galaxy Structure

The known galaxy consists of 42 quadrants — scattered pockets of civilization, not a grid, not a map, but a web of isolated nodes connected by fold corridors. Some quadrants are densely connected. Some are accessible only through a single corridor. Some lie beyond all charted routes entirely, reachable only by those willing to overdrive their fold drives into uncharted void.

Each quadrant contains one or two clusters, each cluster containing two to five star systems. Every galaxy is procedurally generated from a seed — a four-part code assigned at registration. The seed determines everything: quadrant names, system names, station locations, faction presence, veydrite deposits, corridor connections, hazard ratings. Two pilots with different seeds inhabit entirely different galaxies.

The seed is not metaphorical. The Guild's deep mapping engines require a starting value from which to derive the full structure of local space. In practice, this means that no two registration events produce the same galaxy. The contractor who registered before you and the contractor who registers after you are each alone in their own version of the dark. The Guild finds this philosophically unremarkable.

Known and Unknown Space

At registration, the Guild issues a partial corridor chart — roughly 33% of fold connections are pre-loaded into your terminal's navigation memory. The remaining 67% are dark. You know they exist. You do not know where they lead. The galaxy reveals itself as you move through it: folding into a new quadrant reveals all corridors emanating from that position. Deepscan operations can detect corridor signatures in adjacent space. Guild data purchases can provide named connection records. Salvaged logs sometimes contain navigation data. Contacts mention corridors they have used.

The guild does not consider the partial chart a deficiency. They consider it an invitation.

Quadrant States

Each quadrant carries a civilization state that determines station density, encounter frequency, veydrite pricing, faction mix, and hazard levels. States propagate — collapse spreads like a disease. A Collapsed quadrant pulls its Declining neighbors toward collapse at a 40% rate per generation. Declining pulls Contested at 25%. Established space creates pressure, but recovery is pocketed, fragile, and rare. The galaxy trends in one direction.

The Xeno Question

The Guild's official position is that certain anomalous survey findings remain under active review. This document will not elaborate further. Contractors operating in systems flagged with anomalous sensor returns are advised to complete their survey quickly and file their data. The Guild is particularly interested in astrographic records from such systems. The premium reflects this interest.

FIELD NOTE If something in a system does not resolve on sensors, do not attempt repeated resolution sweeps. Log the anomaly. Leave the system. File your data. The Guild will handle classification. You are a surveyor, not an investigator.
SECTION 02
THE ASSAYER'S GUILD

The Assayer's Guild is the oldest continuously operating institution in known space. It predates the current collapse cycle by several centuries and has survived every previous one. Its longevity is not accidental. The Guild survives because every other faction — Pelk Logistics, Colonial Colonies Command, feral settlements, independent operators — needs what the Guild provides: neutral monetary infrastructure and authoritative cartographic records. The Guild does not take sides. It takes percentages.

The Guild's neutrality is its entire value. The moment it favors one corp over another, it loses the thing that makes the galaxy function around it. Every major faction in known space spends considerable political energy ensuring the Guild never has reason to weaponize its position. The Guild is aware of this dynamic. The Guild finds it a satisfactory arrangement.

What the Guild Does

The Guild performs three core functions: it maintains the monetary exchange system (Veydrite Dust pricing, Corp Scrip exchange, currency certification), it maintains the cartographic record (astrographic data collection, star chart distribution, fold corridor mapping, survey certification), and it maintains the contractor network (registration, contract facilitation, reputation tracking).

As a registered independent contractor, you operate on Guild-issued credentials. Your terminal connects to the Guild network. Your astrographic data sells to Guild archive stations. Your reputation score is maintained in Guild ledgers. Your fold corridor data — when you file it — is incorporated into the Guild's cartographic record. You are, in a meaningful sense, a Guild instrument deployed into space at no cost to themselves, because you bear all the operational risk.

The Guild does not think of contractors this way, officially. Officially, contractors are valued independent partners whose contributions to the cartographic record are essential to Guild operations. This is also true. Both things can be true simultaneously. The Guild is comfortable with this.
SECTION 03
CURRENCY AND ECONOMY

Veydrite Dust

Veydrite Dust is the bedrock commodity of the known economy — and, as of the Veydric Fold Drive's widespread adoption, the bedrock of propulsion as well. It occurs in trace quantities around certain stellar classes — O and B-class stars produce the richest deposits — and is extracted through field salvage operations. Veydrite does not corrode, does not decay, cannot be synthesized, and has verified mass. These properties make it the only universally accepted store of value in a galaxy where institutional trust is eroding along with everything else.

The tension every contractor faces: sell veydrite for scrip, or use it to go somewhere. This is not a metaphorical tension. It is an operational one. Manage it accordingly.

SECTION 05
THE VEYDRIC FOLD DRIVE

The Veydric Fold Drive is the mechanism by which vessels transit between quadrants. It does not travel faster than light. It travels shorter. Early survey teams, operating in veydrite-rich stellar environments, documented measurable spacetime density differentials in high-veydrite fields. The Fold Drive exploits these differentials to compress transit geometry — folding the space between origin and destination until they are effectively adjacent, transiting, then releasing. The math is old Guild science. The hardware is increasingly hard to maintain as manufacturing capacity collapses.

Pilots colloquially refer to inter-quadrant transit as "folding" or "a fold." The verb is accurate in ways that make physicists uncomfortable and Guild bureaucrats reach for the official terminology guidelines. The guidelines have not resolved the issue. Contractors continue to say "folding." The Guild continues to write "Veydric Fold Transit" in official documents. This has been the state of affairs for approximately sixty years.

Veydric Fold Cells

Veydric Fold Cells are the primary fuel medium for the Fold Drive. They are machined, refined, and standardized — approximately the size and mass of a reactor rod, slotted into the drive housing one at a time. The Guild manufactures them to specification. Stations stock them at prices that vary by faction affiliation and your standing with that faction. They are what the drive is designed to run on: clean fold, predictable geometry, no drive wear.

The Emergency Reserve — Raw Veydrite

Your vessel maintains a separate emergency reserve tank — a secondary feed system that accepts raw, unrefined veydrite directly from salvage operations. The emergency reserve holds a maximum of 15 kg. It is not elegant. It is not recommended as a primary fuel source. It exists because the people who designed the Light Surveyor understood that contractors operating in Collapsed space cannot always find a station.

The emergency feed converts 10 kg of raw veydrite into one cell equivalent. The drive runs hotter. The fold geometry is less precise. There is a 15% chance of minor Drive subsystem wear per feed operation. Like running a reactor on unprocessed ore — technically functional, not recommended as a lifestyle.

Highway Corridors

Every galaxy contains three to four highway corridors — fold connections between quadrants whose geometry has been so thoroughly pre-calculated by Guild survey vessels that the drive barely has to work. Two cells instead of three. Faster transit. No additional risk. Highway corridors exist between historically high-traffic quadrants — Established space, major trade routes, Guild administrative connections. They are displayed on the fold map as ═══ rather than ───. Find them. Use them. Plan routes through them when possible.

The Blind Fold — Overdrive Transit

The blind fold is not a navigation technique. It is a last resort. It commits the Fold Drive to transit across uncharted void — space where no corridor geometry exists, where the drive must generate its own fold field from scratch rather than following a pre-calculated path. Twelve cells. Drive must be at NOMINAL condition. Something will happen. What happens is not fully under your control.

SECTION 06
NAVIGATION AND TRAVERSAL

Movement is the foundation of contractor operations. There are two levels of transit: movement within your current quadrant, and movement between quadrants via the Fold Drive. Both consume resources. Both advance the day counter. Both carry risk. Plan before committing.

The Fold Corridor Map — map

Before going anywhere, consult the fold map. The map command displays your current position, all known corridors from your current quadrant with their types and cell costs, and a complete list of all quadrants in your current knowledge network. Unknown quadrants — those beyond charted corridors — appear only as a count. The map is your primary navigation reference. Use it before every fold decision.

Corridor display symbols: ═══ highway corridor (2 cells), ─── primary corridor (3 cells), ··· secondary corridor (4 cells). Unknown corridors display a fold signature flavor line — you know something is there, but not what or where it leads.

Galaxy Overview — galaxy

The galaxy command displays all quadrants currently in your knowledge network — those you have visited or that are connected to your position via known corridors. Unknown quadrants do not appear. The list grows as you explore. Your current position is marked with a ► indicator.

Quadrant Survey — scan <1-42>

Passive sensor sweep of a numbered quadrant. Returns system names, star classes, beacon presence, and ◆ markers on charted systems. Does not consume power. Does not generate astrographic data. It tells you what exists in that quadrant. Nothing more.

Folding Between Quadrants — fold <quadrant name>

The primary method of inter-quadrant transit. Specify a destination quadrant by name. The terminal will confirm the corridor type, cell cost, destination state, and notable feature before asking for confirmation. Type yes to initiate the fold sequence or anything else to cancel.

The fold sequence is not instantaneous. Geometry calculation takes 12 seconds for a standard fold, 18 for a blind fold. The countdown runs with real-time flavor text. This is expected. This is not a fast game. The drive is doing genuine work.

IMPORTANT — EXPLICIT CONFIRMATION REQUIRED The fold command will never execute without a yes confirmation. You will never accidentally fold into the void. This is by design. The galaxy is dangerous enough without the terminal making unilateral propulsion decisions.

Overdrive Fold — blindfold <quadrant name>

Transit to a quadrant with no charted corridor connection. Requires 12 fold cells and a Drive subsystem at NOMINAL condition. If the terminal detects that a charted corridor exists to your destination, it will redirect you to the standard fold command. The blind fold is only available for genuinely unconnected destinations. See Section 05 for the full risk table.

Within-Quadrant Transit — nav and jump

nav <system name> plots a calculated course to any named system in the galaxy. Costs fuel and Power Core units. Advances the day counter by 4–11 days. An encounter check triggers on arrival. The terminal will refuse nav transit if fuel is insufficient — it will not warn you when you are approaching the margin.

jump <system name> initiates a blind cluster jump to a system in your current quadrant. Less fuel, fewer days (1–3), elevated encounter probability due to uncomputed arrival vector. Useful for rapid local traversal.

Corridor Discovery

Dark corridors become known through several methods: folding into a new quadrant reveals all corridors from that position; deepscan operations detect corridor signatures in adjacent space; Guild data purchases can provide named connection records at Guild station trade terminals; salvaged ruin logs occasionally contain navigation data; resolved contacts sometimes mention specific corridors they have used. The galaxy reveals itself to those who move through it.

SECTION 10
SALVAGE OPERATIONS

Field salvage is the primary source of Veydrite for contractors without pre-established extraction contracts. It is also the primary source of emergency reserve veydrite — which means salvage has direct propulsive value beyond its market price. A full emergency reserve is a fold you can make when there are no stations nearby. Do not treat it as incidental.

Initiating Salvage — salvage

Salvage is available in systems containing ruin sites, veydrite deposits, or debris fields. Each system can be salvaged once per visit. Salvage yields automatically fill the emergency reserve to 15 kg first, then add the remainder to the cargo hold as sellable veydrite.

Hazard level affects yield variance and incident probability during operations. High-hazard systems yield more — if you survive the operation. Xeno-tainted systems have a chance of anomalous finds.

Ruin Logs — scan log

Ruin sites can be scanned for data logs. Recovered logs are added to your record archive and viewable with logs. They are flavor artifacts: crew records, manifests, station closure notices, personal accounts. Some contain navigation data that reveals specific dark corridors. These are mechanically significant finds. Read your logs.

SECTION 11
EXTRACTION OPERATIONS

Extraction operations are the deliberate, systematic removal of ore, ice compounds, and exotic materials from planetary bodies. Unlike salvage — which is opportunistic, fast, and dependent on what wreckage left behind — extraction is deliberate, slow, and dependent on what the planet actually contains. The yield is different. The tooling is different. The planning requirement is substantially higher.

The ancient civilizations whose ruins you are salvaging were themselves extractors. On a scale that has not been replicated since. What you find in the ground is the concentrated residue of an industrial operation that was interrupted by whatever ended them. The ore was already halfway processed before you arrived. The Guild calls these sites Industrial Remnant Sites. Pilots call them veins. The value is not academic.

EXTRACTION DOCTRINE — AUGER-1 Extraction operations require the Auger-1 Light Mining Head installed in your utility slot. The Auger-1 cannot perform salvage operations. The Harrow-7 cannot perform geological survey or extraction. Your vessel carries one utility slot. Choose accordingly, or carry the second tool in cargo and swap at stations.

The Extraction Sequence

Extraction operations follow a mandatory sequential protocol. Each step gates the next. There is no shortcut.

Step 1 — where: Mark the system as visited. Required before geological survey. The Auger-1 needs positional and astrographic context before it will commit power to a geological sweep. This is standard survey doctrine, not a software limitation.

Step 2 — survey node: Broad-spectrum geological sweep of all bodies in the system. Costs 35 Power Core units. Advances the day counter by 1. Returns each body's type, deposit quality (RICH / STANDARD / DEPLETED), ore grade category (EXOTIC / RARE / UNCOMMON / COMMON), and estimated yield in units.

MASS ANOMALY NOTATION Occasionally, survey node will flag a body with the notation: Anomalous mass reading. Verify on body survey. This notation indicates a potential NPCP contamination event. See The Ash section below. Do not ignore this notation.

Step 3 — survey body <n>: Targeted geological sweep of a specific body by index number. Costs 15 Power Core units. Advances the day counter by 1. Returns full ore composition — every ore type present, quantity in units, extraction quality, and the refined metal each ore produces. Required before extraction can begin on that body.

Step 4 — mine body <n> or mine body <n> <cycles>: Initiate extraction. Costs 25 Power Core units per cycle. Advances the day counter by 1–2 days per cycle. Multiple cycles can be queued in a single command. Extracted ore transfers to your ore pods. If a pod reaches capacity mid-operation, extraction halts and the terminal notifies you.

The Ash — NPCP Contamination

The Null-Propagating Crystalline Parasite — The Ash — is a self-propagating mineral process first documented in Guild survey records approximately ninety years ago. It is not alive in any conventional sense. It is a crystalline structure that catalyzes the conversion of surrounding ore into a stable, inert compound with no extractable value. Pilots who have extracted from contaminated nodes describe the output as ore-colored powder that compacts on contact.

The Ash cannot be detected by standard geological sweep. Body survey may flag a mass anomaly — a reading that is subtly wrong in a way the array cannot fully characterize. This is the only warning you will get. If you extract from a contaminated node, The Ash may spread to adjacent bodies in the system. The spread probability is low per cycle. It compounds.

The Guild pays a significant bounty for confirmed Ash reports filed at any Guild station. More than the ore would have been worth. The Guild does not explain why it is specifically interested in these reports. The Guild's behavior in response to confirmed contamination events suggests the interest is not academic.

NPCP PROTOCOL If extraction yields Ash Compactate, stop operations immediately. Do not run additional cycles. Dock at the nearest Guild station and file an Ash report. The bounty is substantial. Continued extraction spreading contamination to additional bodies is not recoverable. The Guild has not published data on systems that underwent full NPCP propagation. The absence of data is its own notation.

Weapon Condition

Hull reaching zero is death. Your save file is deleted. Your pilot record is entered into the graveyard. There is no continue from last checkpoint. There is no reload. The void does not give back.

The Guild records the death of every registered contractor. The record includes cause of death, day of death, last known location, and achievement log. This information is retained in the Guild archive indefinitely. The Guild does not consider this morbid. It considers it accurate.

SECTION 17
THE GUILD RECORD ARCHIVE

The Guild Record Archive is the longitudinal operational record of a registered contractor. It logs notable events — first operations, standing changes, combat outcomes, survey milestones, economic achievements — and retains them for the duration of the contractor's active registration and beyond. The archive does not delete entries. It only adds them.

Access the archive in-game with the record command. Access it from the main menu via the Guild Record Archive link. Records are organized by category and expand on click to display full detail including the day the event occurred.

The Guild maintains these records not out of sentiment, but because they constitute the most accurate longitudinal dataset of independent contractor operations in known space. Contractors have occasionally asked why the Guild cares how many systems they have charted or whether they survived a combat engagement at 15% hull. The Guild's answer is consistent: accurate data is always useful. The galaxy is large. The Guild wants to know what is in it, and who managed to come back.

The Graveyard

When a contractor dies, their record is finalized and transferred to the permanent archive. Their vessel's last known position, cause of death, day count, and complete achievement log are preserved. Dead pilots are not forgotten. They are filed. There is a distinction, and the Guild considers it important.

SECTION 18
SURVIVAL NOTES

This section contains operational guidance extracted from contractor debrief records, incident reports, and post-mortem analysis. Most of it is obvious in retrospect.

Fold Cell Discipline

You start with 3 fold cells. The magazine holds 20. Three cells gets you one inter-quadrant fold and almost nothing else. Buy cells at every station where your standing allows it. Do not leave Established space without a full magazine if you can avoid it. Do not enter Collapsed space without planning your exit. The cell count in the sidebar is not decorative — it is the number of escapes you have left.

Emergency Reserve Management

The emergency reserve fills automatically during salvage and mining, before veydrite goes to the cargo hold. Keep it full. A full 15 kg reserve is one emergency fold available regardless of station access. In Collapsed space, that one fold may be the difference between finding a Feral settlement and drifting. The command is emergency refine <n> — 10 kg per cell, 15% drive wear risk. In the scenario where you need it, you do not have better options.

Survey Before Mining

The extraction sequence is mandatory and sequential: where, then survey node, then survey body <n>, then mine body <n>. Each step gates the next. Attempting to shortcut this sequence wastes power and returns errors. There is no shortcut. The Auger-1 needs to know what it is drilling into before it drills.

Watch for The Ash

Body surveys that flag mass anomalies should be treated as contamination warnings, not measurement errors. If extraction yields Ash Compactate — ore-colored powder, zero market value — stop immediately. Running additional cycles because you already committed the power is how one contaminated body becomes four. File the Guild report. Take the bounty. Leave.

The Highway

Every galaxy has 3–4 highway corridors — pre-calculated fold geometry, 2 cells instead of 3. Find them early. Build routes through them. A contractor who has identified the highway network and routes through it will always outlast one who burns primary corridors everywhere. Check the fold map for ═══ markings from every quadrant you visit.

Power Management

Dock when your core is low. Shore power is free with the docking fee. Running a deep survey operation on 35 power will not work. Running into an encounter on 10 power will end badly. Plan power usage before committing to operations.

Hull Monitoring

The hull bar in the sidebar turns orange below 30%. This is a warning, not a safe threshold. At 20% hull, a single missed evade or a bad combat exchange ends your pilot. Repair early. Repair often. Repair before going into contested space, not after.

Don't Blindfold Into Nothing

The blind fold requires 12 cells. It has a 5% chance of destroying your Drive and a 10% chance of landing you in the wrong system with a CRITICAL Drive. Before committing to a blind fold, ask: do I know anything is in that quadrant? Do I have enough hull to survive a bad outcome? Do I have the scrip to repair a destroyed Drive if I somehow reach a station? If the answer to any of these is no, the blind fold is not a navigation decision. It is a gamble. The void does not honor gamblers.

Contract Deadlines

Contracts have deadlines generated based on realistic travel time plus a 20% margin. The margin exists, but it is not generous. Do not accept a contract and then spend four days doing salvage runs in the opposite direction. The deadline clock does not care about your detours. It does not care about your fold cell shortage. It does not care.

The Xeno Presence

Some systems will present readings that do not resolve. Some contacts will not identify. Some logs will describe things that should not be there. The Guild's advice is consistent: log it, file it, leave. The Guild is specifically interested in this data and pays accordingly. Your curiosity, however understandable, is not worth your pilot.

The Guild has reviewed 847 contractor incident reports involving anomalous sensor returns over the past forty years. Of these, 312 involved the contractor attempting extended engagement with the anomaly. The outcome data for this subset is not published. The Guild considers the aggregate survival rate sufficient guidance.
